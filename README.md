# Weather Impact on Flights Operations
### This is Weather Impact on Flights Operations project.


_"When Storms Hit, Data Keeps You Flying."_


## Project Overview
This project is a two-view interactive Excel and Power Bi dashboard that an  alyzes 10,000 flights across multiple travel classes, travel purposes, and a multi-year timeline (2023–2025). It isolates the operational and commercial impact of weather disruptions versus normal operations — revealing not just what goes wrong in bad weather, but how well airlines actually manage it.This project is designed for airline operations managers, revenue analysts, customer experience teams, and C-suite executives who need a clear, data-backed picture of weather-related operational risk and its downstream effect on passenger trust and business performance.

This project analyzes how adverse weather conditions affect airline operations, passenger satisfaction, and business performance. The dataset covers 10,000 flights, of which 1,507 (15%) were impacted by weather disruptions.

The dashboard provides a holistic view of operational resilience:
    - **On-Time Performance**: Despite weather challenges, airlines maintained a 77% on-time rate.
    - **Cancellations**: Only 83 flights (<1%) were cancelled due to weather, showing effective contingency planning.
    - **Passenger Satisfaction**: Ratings dropped slightly during bad weather (6.8 vs. 7.0), but remained above industry benchmarks.
    - **No-Show Behavior**: Weather increased no-shows, particularly among business and emergency travelers.
    - **Delay Trends**: Weather-related delays averaged 8–12 minutes, with seasonal peaks during monsoon and winter months.

This project demonstrates how aviation analytics can guide operational planning, customer experience management, and strategic investment in resilience.

# Key Insights
1) Weather Impacts Only 15% of Flights — But That 15% Matters Enormously
    - Out of 10,000 total flights, only 1,507 (15%) were affected by adverse weather conditions.
    - This means 85% of operations run unaffected — a strong baseline of operational resilience.
    - However, the financial, reputational, and passenger experience costs concentrated in that 15% make it the most important segment to optimize.
    - The daily average of ~10.26 weather-impacted flights confirms disruptions are a routine operational challenge, not a rare event — requiring systematic, not ad hoc, responses.
    - **Actionable**: Weather disruption management should be treated as a core operational competency, not an exception protocol.


2) 77% On-Time Rate Under Weather Conditions — Resilient, But Room to Grow
    - Even during adverse weather, the airline maintains a 77% on-time performance rate — a figure that compares favorably against global aviation benchmarks where weather-impacted on-time rates often fall below 70%.
    - This indicates the airline has effective proactive re-routing and recovery strategies already in place.
    - The remaining 23% of weather-impacted flights that are delayed represent the optimization frontier — where predictive scheduling and buffer time can make the biggest difference.
    - **Actionable**: Closing the gap from 77% to 85%+ on-time performance during weather events should be a defined OKR for the operations team.


3) Cancellations Are Minimal — Delays Are the Real Disruptor
    - Only 83 flights were cancelled due to weather — less than 1% of total flights.
    - Of all flights during bad weather periods, 94.70% (9,470 flights) were NOT cancelled, with only 5.30% (530 flights) being cancelled.
    - This clearly shows the airline's operational philosophy: absorb the disruption through delays, not cancellations — which is the right call for passenger experience and revenue protection.
    - **Actionable**: The low cancellation rate is a competitive differentiator and should be communicated in brand messaging. However, delay management must be tightened to ensure that "not cancelled" also means "minimally delayed."


4) Flight Delay Trend — Predictable Seasonal Spikes
    - Weather-related delays fluctuate between 8–12 minutes, with visible peaks during specific months.
    - Seasonal spikes clearly align with monsoon and winter weather periods, which are forecastable well in advance.
    - Flights with no weather impact maintain consistently stable, lower delay durations across the same timeline — confirming that the delay variance is weather-driven, not structural.
    - **Actionable**: Since delay peaks are seasonal and predictable, airlines can build pre-emptive scheduling buffers during known high-risk months — reducing cascading delays without adding significant operational cost.


5) Passenger Satisfaction Drops Only 0.2 Points in Bad Weather — A Remarkable Result
    - Average satisfaction scores: 7.0 (no weather impact) vs 6.8 (weather impact) — a drop of just 0.2 points.
    - This minimal gap is a testament to effective passenger communication, disruption handling, and maintained trust even during challenging conditions.
    - A score of 6.8 during adverse weather remains above acceptable aviation industry benchmarks (typically 6.5+), indicating passengers understand weather disruptions but still expect — and are receiving — professional handling.
    - Actionable: The goal should be to defend the 0.2 gap and ideally narrow it further. Pushing weather-impact satisfaction from 6.8 to 7.0+ through proactive rebooking and compensation would be a measurable customer experience win.


6) No-Show Behavior — 6% Weather Impact No-Show Rate
    - The weather impact no-show rate is 6%, which is relatively low but represents a revenue leakage and scheduling complexity issue.
    - No-show breakdown by travel purpose during weather impact: Business (30), Emergency (19), Family (21), Leisure (17) — with Business travelers showing the highest no-show count even in non-weather conditions (95).
    - This suggests Business travelers no-show most often, likely due to meeting cancellations or last-minute schedule changes — and weather compounds this behavior.
    - Actionable: Targeted pre-flight outreach (automated rebooking options, flexible change policies) for Business travelers during weather alerts can reduce no-shows and protect seat revenue.


# Business Recommendations (Recommendations for Management)
  - Seasonal Buffering: Introduce buffer times during monsoon/winter months to reduce delay spikes.
  - Predictive Analytics: Invest in forecasting tools to anticipate and mitigate weather disruptions.
  - Customer Communication: Enhance real-time alerts and proactive rebooking to maintain satisfaction above 7.0.
  - Targeted Resilience: Prioritize business and emergency travelers with flexible policies and compensation.
  - Operational Optimization: Use weather insights to optimize crew scheduling and aircraft allocation.
